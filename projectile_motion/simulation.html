Projectile Motion

Projectile motion possesses many interesting properties. One of them is the fact that two projectiles, when thrown with the same speed but at angles θ and 90 - θ respectively, land at the same position! You can verify this fact by using the interactive simulation of projectile motion given below. Also, try to hit that blue target!





Basic concepts: \[ x = u \cos \theta \] \[ y = ut \sin \theta - \frac{1}{2} g t^2 \]
  1. In projectile motion, acceleration due to gravity acts in the vertical direction. Hence, only the y-component of velocity is affected by acceleration due to gravity. The x-component of velocity remains constant. Observe that the value of vx remains constant in the above simulation
  2. Let the coordinates of the projectile be denoted by (x,y). Let the initial speed be u = √(ux2 + uy2). Let θ be the angle that the vector u makes with the X-axis.
  3. We can determine the (x,y) position of the projectile in a parametric fashion: x = ut*cos θ, where 't' is the time since the projectile has been launched and thereafter remained in air. y = ut*sin θ - 0.5 gt2. By eliminating 't', we can obtain an equation in terms of x, y, u and θ only. The form of this equation resembles that of a parabola. Hence, a projectile follows a parabolic trajectory
  4. During ascent, vy is positive. During descent vy is negative. At the highest point, vy is zero!
Time of ascent, descent and flight: \[ t_f = \frac{2u}{g} \] \[ t_a = t_d = \frac{u}{g} \]
  1. Using the fact that vy is zero at the highest point, and using v = u - gt in the y-direction, we obtain the fact that time of ascent = time of descent = u*sin θ / g and time of flight = 2u*sin θ / g
  2. Time of ascent = time of descent provided that air resistance is absent/being ignored. All calculations here make the same assumption
Maximum height: \[ H_{max} = \frac{u^2 \sin \theta}{2g} \]
  1. Using the fact that vy is zero at the highest point, and using v2= u2 - 2gy in the y-direction, we get maximum height H = (u*sin θ)2/ 2g
  2. For a given initial velocity u, maximum possible height is achieved when angle of projection is 90 degrees, as is evident from the above formula
Range: \[ R = \frac{u^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} \]
  1. We know that x = ut*cos θ. If we subtitute 't' as the time of flight, we obtain the horizontal displacement of the projectile, denoted by R: 2u2sin θ cos θ / g = u2sin 2θ / g
  2. As can be deciphered from the above formula, range is maximum when θ = 45 degrees
  3. You can verify it here that sin (180 - A) = sin A;, hence the range is same for angles of projection of θ and 90 - θ, provided the initial velocity remains constant
Notes:
  1. The above simulation is fully compatible with SI units. Acceleration due to gravity = 9.81 acceleration units
  2. The length of the canvas corresponds to (maximum possible range + 40) length units. Maximum range is achieved when θ = 45 degrees and u = 100 velocity units
