Ising Model

The Ising model is used to model a system of interacting spins in a ferromagnetic material. Spins are distributed in a connected lattice. Each spin is randomly set to +1 or -1. When temperature is low and interaction strength is positive, the system evolves towards the lowest energy configuration.




(changing grid size automatically restarts the simulation)

Description

The energy of a lattice is calculated using the following formula: \[ E = - J \sum_{\lt ij \gt} s_i s_j \] In every Monte-Carlo step, a random spin is chosen and flipped. The change in energy of the system (\( \Delta E \)) is calculated. If energy has decreased, then the flip is accepted. If energy has increased, then the flip is accepted with a probability of: \[ P = \exp\left(\frac{-\Delta E}{k_B T}\right) \] This set of steps is known as the Metropolis algorithm. Higher temperature encourages unfavourable spins, thereby increasing disorder of the system, whereas a negative interaction strength \( J \) seeks to increase energy.



Note:
  1. The boundary condition is periodic.
  2. This simulation may hang on lower-end devices, for large grid sizes and large values of MC steps.
